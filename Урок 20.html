Урок 20


This chapter deals with major subject of Russian semantics - the verbs of motion. This subject is one of the hards for non-native speakers to grasp because it's a concept that most languages, English, included do not have. The key distinction that allows the speaker to choose the right motion verb is whether a given motion involves multiple directions or a single direction.


At its heart, the concept of the motion verbs is illustrated by the following sentences:


Она идёт в школу. She is going to school.  (Meaning, she is currently walking to school.)

Она ходит в школу. She goes to school. (Meaning, she attends school.)


With that in mind, realize that the presentation in NPRC is pretty weak. Partly, it's because the subject doesn't lend itself well to chapter-length presentations. This is a subject that needs crystal clear presentation and lots and lots of practice. The presentation in NPRC could be improved dramatically by deferring the presentation on the perfective formation of the verbs of motion until after that basic semantics are well-practiced first.




Fundamental advice for this chapter


Before attacking the nuances of this complex subject of Russian grammar, I would recommend memorizing the 14 unprefixed imperfective verbs of motion and practice their conjugations repeatedly until it becomes completely natural.

20.3 Where aspect meets the verbs of motion


This can be tricky to understand.


The 14 verb pairs in the table at the beginning of the chapter are all imperfective, but all also have a perfective partner made by prefixing по-. This means that there are 4 forms for each verbal concept. The effect of the prefix по- with multidirectional verbs means "a little bit", "for a little while", etc. Basically a diminutive. For unidirectional verbs, it means either "to start" or "to make a complete trip in one direction."


по- prefixation with multidirectional verbs:


1. Давай походим по театру. "Let's take a walk around the theatre." i.e. let's walk around for a little while. The по-- prefix here, again, makes a sort of diminutive.


по-- prefixation with unidirectional verbs:


1. Пойдём! - "Let's go." In this case, the prefix gives the verb the "to start" or "to set off" meaning.

2. Где сестра? Она пошла в театр.  "Where is your sister? She's gone to the theatre." Here, the prefix implies a single round trip.


To make matters even more complicated, you can get the same "one round trip" effect with multidirectional verbs using yet a different prefix с-.


1. Я сбегаю в мазазин за хлебом. "I'll run to the store (and back) for some bread


Supplementary books on verbs of motion


Verbs of Motion in Russian, L. Murayova - I have a very old edition of this classic text and it is well used.  The old school illustrations actually do aid in understanding the concepts. The book is recommended; but make sure you have enough vocabulary at your disposal to make it worthwhile.


A Comprehensive Russian Grammar, Terence Wade - this has a succinct but thorough section on the verbs of motion.  It does a better job distinguishing between the semantics of unidirectional vs. multidirectional verbs in otherwise similar contexts. 


Supplementary video presentations on the verbs of motion:


  • идти/ходить, part I - this is a somewhat slow video but does a good job contrasting the use cases for идти and ходить
  • итди/ходить, part II - more compare/contrast between the use of идти and ходить
  • Verbs of motion, part I - this is more just a bunch of examples but graphics are good
  • Verbs of motion, part 1, Introduction - (drpedsen) a very well-organized clear and concise presentation. His Russian pronunciation isn't very good, but the explanation is fairly well-done.
