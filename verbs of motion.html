Introduction and basics


Russian makes a distinction between verbs of motion that imply unidirectional vs. multidirectional movement. For example, consider the verb(s) for "to go on foot", there are two separate verbs:


идти - means to go in a single direction or non-habitually.

ходить - means to go in multiple directions or habitually.


Куда вы идете? - Where are you going? The person asking the question wants to know where the respondent is going right now - a unidirectional motion.


Ваша дочка ходит в школу? - Does your daughter go to school? Here, we're asking about an habitual act. We're not asking if she's walking to school right now. We're asking if she goes to school habitually.


In the case of English, "to go" is a rather non-specific verb. English speakers rely on context and other clarifications to convey additional meaning. Instead, Russian speakers rely more on the verb form itself to convey this additional meaning. In some ways, it's like the additional meaning conveyed by the choice of aspect. In the case of verbs of motion, Russian makes a more nuanced distinction about the direction of motion (unidirectional vs. multidirectional.) The same verb choice is also governed by whether a motion is habitual or singular.


The other distinctive feature of Russian verbs of motion is the prefixation of base verbs to apply additional semantic nuance.

Rules governing the choice of unidirectional vs. multidirectional verbs


The basic rules are discussed here but are covered in more depth in the section on the choice of multidirectional or unidirectional verbs.


  1. If the motion involves multiple directions, use the multidirectional form.
  2. If there is no motion, use the multidirectional form.
  3. If the number of directions isn't relevant, use the multidirectional form.
