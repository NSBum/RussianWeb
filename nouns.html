Nouns


Nouns in Russian have three distinct differences from nouns in English:


  1. They are declined, meaning that the endings change based on their role in the sentence. Much more on this later.
  2. They have a gender, either masculine, feminine, or neuter
  3. They aren't associated with an article. That's right - there's no word for "a", "an", "the", etc.




Russian has 6 cases: nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, instrumental and preopositive cases.



Functions of the cases in Russian


1. The nominative case in Russian answers questions about "who?" or "what?". When listing the declensions, it is always the intial form. It's what the dictionary gives. 


Example: студент читает (The student is reading.)



2. The genitive case indicates possession. Something belongs to someone or something else. We might use "of" in English or apostrophe-s to indicate this.


Example: собаки яблоко (The dog's apple.)



3. The dative case designates that some is given or addressed to the person or object. 


Example: Я даю это другу   (I give it to the friend.)



4. The accusative case is object (direct object) of an action.


Example:  Я купила собаку. (I bought a dog.)



5. The instumental case is used to mean an instrument (agent) that helps make or do something.


Example: Я пишу карандашом   (I write with a pencil.)



6. The prepositional case is used with certain prepositions.


Example: Цветы стоят на столе   (The flowers are on the table.)
