21.2 Свой


Свой is a possessive pronoun that refers back to the subject of the sentence. In English it would be "own." It helps eliminate some of the ambiguity that can exist in English, as in the following sentence:


Mark said John has his ticket.


Clearly John has at least one ticket, but to whom does the ticket belong? Is it John's? Mark's? Свой in part eliminates that ambiguity. See the middle of page 258 for the details.


The declension of свой is just like твой and friends:



Some of the "rules" for using свой:


  1. When the subject is ты, свой is preferred rather than твой.
  2. Russians often omit pronouns, including possessive pronouns. If the possession is obvious you can probably omit the possessive pronoun, including свой.
  3. From the NPRC, page 258: "If the subject is 3rd person, you must use свой if the thing belongs to the subject."
  4. Don't use свой if the thing being possessed in in a different clause. Russian is more explicit about demarcating clauses with commas and the presence of что, где, etc. is a tip-off.
  5. The author goes to great links to say that свой must not be used in the nominative case (ALL CAPS!) but then goes on (pg. 259) to list several exceptions to the rule.
  6. The nominative use of свой is indicated when the meaning is "one's own" in cases of possessive construction, e.g. У каждого своё мнение. Everyone has his own opinion.
  7. The nominative use of свой is also indicated when the intent is to mean "home-made" Хлеб свой, не из магазина. The bread is home-made, not from the store.
  8. Finally, свой can refer to a subject that's not nominative as in impersonal constructions, e.g. Мне надо найти свой билет. I have to find my ticket. (Personally, I wonder if the characterization of мне here as the subject is correct. Other references, such as this indicate that impersonal constructions have no subject.)
