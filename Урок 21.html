21.2 Свой


Свой is a possessive pronoun that refers back to the subject of the sentence. In English it would be "own." It helps eliminate some of the ambiguity that can exist in English, as in the following sentence:


Mark said John has his ticket.


Clearly John has at least one ticket, but to whom does the ticket belong? Is it John's? Mark's? Свой in part eliminates that ambiguity. See the middle of page 258 for the details.


The declension of свой is just like твой and friends:



Some of the "rules" for using свой:


  1. When the subject is ты, свой is preferred rather than твой.
  2. Russians often omit pronouns, including possessive pronouns. If the possession is obvious you can probably omit the possessive pronoun, including свой.
  3. From the NPRC, page 258: "If the subject is 3rd person, you must use свой if the thing belongs to the subject."
  4. Don't use свой if the thing being possessed in in a different clause. Russian is more explicit about demarcating clauses with commas and the presence of что, где, etc. is a tip-off.
  5. The author goes to great links to say that свой must not be used in the nominative case (ALL CAPS!) but then goes on (pg. 259) to list several exceptions to the rule.
  6. The nominative use of свой is indicated when the meaning is "one's own" in cases of possessive construction, e.g. У каждого своё мнение. Everyone has his own opinion.
  7. The nominative use of свой is also indicated when the intent is to mean "home-made" Хлеб свой, не из магазина. The bread is home-made, not from the store.
  8. Finally, свой can refer to a subject that's not nominative as in impersonal constructions, e.g. Мне надо найти свой билет. I have to find my ticket. (Personally, I wonder if the characterization of мне here as the subject is correct. Other references, such as this indicate that impersonal constructions have no subject.)

21.4 Себя


Closely related to свой is себя, but the latter is a reflexive rather than possessive pronoun. It refers back to the subject of the clause in which it appears (or the agent of nearest verb as Wade puts it more accurately.) 


As for the declension of себя, there is no nominative case because it can never be the subject of the sentence. Otherwise, it is just like тебя.



N.B. whereas with свой it is somewhat optional whether to use it or not, you must use себя when you are referring back to the subject.


21.5 Idioms using себя


These are all useful.


Она пошла к себе. - She went to her (own) room/house.

Он у себя. - He's at home. (He's at his own place.)

Прийти в себя. - To come to one's senses, to recover.

У меня нет при себе денег. - I don't have any money on me.

Он назвал себя. - He gave his name.

ОТ СЕБЯ - PUSH (sign on door)

К СЕБЕ - PULL


21.6 Себя vs. -ся reflexive verbs


This is an interesting question of overlap in Russian semantics because both forms refer to oneself. Sometimes the reflexive verb is only tangentially related to an action reflected on oneself. Nonetheless, the reflexive verb form is usually less emphatic than the себя form. The author uses the following example:


Он считается специалистом. He is considered a specialist.

Он считает себя специалистом. He considers himself a specialist.


It really is just the difference between the strength of active vs. passive voice but is used to introduce purposeful or subjunctive clauses.


21.8 Чтовы


Чтобы is just a mashup of что + бы.


N.B. Чтобы is always followed by the past tense, never with the present or future tenses.


Я хочу, чтобы вы голосовали. - I want you to vote.

Мы хотим, чтобы в мире не было ядерного оружия. - We want the world to be without nuclear weapons.


N.B. It can be used with other words that indicate something is desired. From the A Comprehensive Russian Grammar (Wade):



Grammatically, this use of чтобы is called optative mood. 


This is where the NPRC simplifies things a bit, grammatically-speaking. The end result is the same, so no worries. The Wade grammar separates out clauses that express wish or desire (optative mood) from other forms of the subjunctive mood.
